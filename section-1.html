1 AI vs ML vs Deep Learning

These three words get used interchangeably — but they nest inside each other.

Artificial Intelligence is the biggest circle: any technique that makes a machine act "smart," including old rule-based systems (if temperature > 30, turn on fan) with no learning at all.

Machine Learning sits inside it: instead of writing the rules, you show the machine examples and it figures out the rules itself.

Deep Learning is inside ML: it uses neural networks with many layers. This is what powers today's AI.

Live: the nesting
The key shift: going from AI → ML, you stop writing rules and start providing data.

2 Neural Networks

Loosely inspired by the brain — a web of "neurons" arranged in layers.

Data enters the input layer, flows through hidden layers that transform it, and produces an answer at the output layer. Every connection has a weight. "Learning" just means adjusting those weights until the outputs are correct. Big models have billions of them.

3 Tokens & Tokenization

Models don't read words. They read tokens.

Tokenization chops text into chunks. A token is roughly ¾ of a word (~4 characters). Common words are a single token; rarer words get split into pieces. Everything — pricing, limits, memory — is counted in tokens. Rule of thumb: 1,000 tokens ≈ 750 words.

Live: type something and watch it tokenize

4 Embeddings

Turning meaning into numbers.

Each token becomes a vector — a long list of numbers. The magic: similar meanings end up close together in this space. "King" sits near "queen"; "banana" sits far away. This is how a model grasps that two different words mean related things — and it's the foundation of vector search and RAG.

Why it matters for you: embeddings + vector search are the engine behind most enterprise "chat with your docs" RAG deployments.

5 Transformers & Attention

The 2017 breakthrough ("Attention Is All You Need") that made modern LLMs possible.

The key idea is attention: when the model processes a word, it can look at every other word at once and decide how relevant each one is. That's how it resolves ambiguity.

Live: which words does "bank" pay attention to?

The glowing links show what the focused word "attends to." Notice how bank leans on interest and rates to decide it means a financial bank.

Bonus: because it looks at the whole sentence in parallel (not one word at a time), it trains beautifully on GPUs.

6 Large Language Models (LLMs)

A transformer trained on a huge amount of text to do one deceptively simple thing.

That one thing: predict the next token. That's genuinely it. ChatGPT is, at its core, a very sophisticated autocomplete. Reasoning, translation, coding — all of it emerges from doing next-token prediction extremely well at massive scale.

7 Next-Token Prediction

Watch the "autocomplete" actually happen.

At each step, the model produces a probability for every possible next token, picks one, appends it, and repeats. One token at a time.

8 Temperature, Top-p, Top-k

The knobs that control how the model picks from those probabilities.

Temperature controls randomness. Top-k only considers the k most likely tokens. Top-p considers the smallest group of tokens whose probability adds up past p.

Use low temperature for facts and code; higher for brainstorming.

9 Context Windows

How much the model can "see" at once.

The context window is the max number of tokens the model holds in view — your prompt plus the conversation history plus its own reply. Go over it and the oldest tokens fall off and are forgotten. It's why a very long chat eventually loses track of how it started.

11 Hallucinations

Confident, fluent… and completely wrong.

Because a model predicts plausible text — not verified facts — it will happily invent things and state them with total confidence. It has no built-in sense of "I don't know."

12 Parameters & Quantization

How big is the model, and how do we shrink it?

Parameters are the learned weights. "B" means billion: 7B runs on a laptop, 70B needs serious hardware, 671B is frontier-scale. More parameters ≈ more capability (a rough proxy, not a promise).

Quantization compresses those weights from high precision (16-bit) down to 8- or 4-bit — much smaller and faster, for a tiny accuracy cost.

Why you'll hear it: quantization is what lets a big model run on-prem or on a single GPU — key to sovereign / on-prem AI conversations.

13 Mixture of Experts (MoE)

Big-model quality at small-model running cost.

Instead of one giant network where every weight fires on every token, the model is split into many expert sub-networks. A router picks just a few experts per token. A model can have 671B total parameters but only activate ~37B for any given token.

🎯 How ChatGPT Works

Putting every piece together — this is the goal.

ChatGPT is a large language model built on the transformer architecture. It was trained on a vast amount of text to do one thing: predict the next token.

Your prompt is broken into tokens, converted into embeddings (numeric vectors capturing meaning), and fed through the network — where attention lets it weigh how every token relates to every other. It outputs a probability over the next token; temperature / top-p / top-k decide which one to pick. It appends that token and repeats — one at a time — all within its context window.

Because it predicts plausible text rather than looking up facts, it can hallucinate. And techniques like quantization and Mixture of Experts make these enormous models cheap enough to actually run.
